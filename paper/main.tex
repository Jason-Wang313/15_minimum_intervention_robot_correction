\begin{abstract}
Physical corrections are a natural way to teach robots, but a correction that
repairs the current execution can still be invisible to the learner that
determines future behavior. We formalize a \emph{Minimum Effective Correction}:
the minimum-cost physical intervention whose image through the robot learner's
update channel crosses specified future-behavior boundaries. The resulting
mechanism, Update-Boundary Correction (UBC), solves a local minimum-norm
halfspace problem and returns either a correction, a nullspace warning, or an
infeasibility certificate for the robot's current correction interface. In a
full-scale synthetic suite with eight experiment families and 2,426 seed-row
summaries, UBC obtains 1.000 reachable future success in the main context
coverage setting while current-only correction obtains 0.000; random feasible
search approaches the success rate but uses 2.259 times the physical norm. The
same suite exposes the boundary conditions: at channel-noise $\sigma=0.10$,
unguarded estimated-channel UBC succeeds in 0.344 of reachable cases, while a
guarded variant reaches 0.906 at larger norm; under nonlinear curvature 0.20,
the raw linear certificate has 0.000 true success whereas a trust-recentered
variant reaches 0.817. The evidence supports a mechanism claim: corrections
should be minimized after projection through the learner's update channel. It
does not claim hardware validation, global nonlinear optimality, or solved
human-intent inference.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Robots are often corrected physically. A person nudges an arm away from an
obstacle, drags a waypoint to a safer side of a table, resists a gripper closing
too early, or takes over a shared-control interface for one segment of motion.
These interventions have immediate mechanical effects, and they can also be used
as learning signals. Prior work has shown how demonstrations, corrections,
preferences, interventions, and shared-control signals can train policies,
rewards, objectives, and assistive controllers
\citep{argall2009survey,chernova2014robot,bajcsy2017learning,bajcsy2018learning,
sadigh2017active,ross2011dagger,kelly2019hgdagger}. Yet a subtle gap remains:
the physical correction that looks smallest or most direct in the current
trajectory need not be the smallest correction that the robot learner will
absorb into future behavior.

The distinction is not semantic. Suppose the learner updates parameters
$\theta$ by projecting a correction $c$ through a local update channel $J$.
Future behavior is then measured by margins $m_j(\theta+Jc)$ over future
contexts $z_j$. A correction can be visually salient and still lie in the
nullspace of the composed map from physical effort to future margins. Conversely,
a correction that looks larger in the current state can be the smallest one that
crosses a future decision boundary. Human effort is spent in physical space, but
future learning happens after a robot-specific projection.

This paper studies that projection as the main object. We ask: given a robot
learner, a physical correction interface, and a finite set of future behavior
predicates, what is the smallest physical intervention that changes future
autonomous behavior? The answer is the \emph{Minimum Effective Correction}
(MEC). MEC is not the smallest displacement that repairs the present motion. It
is the smallest correction that is effective under the robot's own update map.

The operational method is Update-Boundary Correction (UBC). In the local affine
case, UBC solves a minimum-norm halfspace problem,
\begin{equation}
    c^\star = \arg\min_c \|c\|_W^2
    \quad\textrm{s.t.}\quad Hc \ge h,
    \label{eq:intro-ubc}
\end{equation}
where $H$ is the composed future-margin/update channel and $h$ is the vector of
required future margin deficits. When the feasible set is nonempty, UBC returns
the minimum-cost physical correction for the modeled channel. When the set is
empty, the current interface cannot certify the desired future behavior. When
the channel has nullspace, physical effort can alter current compliance while
leaving all certified future margins unchanged.

\paragraph{Why another correction paper?}
The closest correction and preference-learning papers ask what the human wants
or how to update a reward from interaction. This paper asks a different
diagnostic question: can this physical correction channel move the learner
across the future boundaries we care about? That question is useful even when a
downstream reward learner is sophisticated, because the physical signal can be
filtered through features, tactile channels, shared-autonomy arbitration,
demonstration buffers, or policy-update rules before it affects future actions.

\paragraph{Contributions.}
First, we define Minimum Effective Correction as a learner-relative object, not a
geometric trajectory edit. Second, we derive UBC, including weighted effort,
infeasibility, nullspace, channel-error, and local nonlinear variants. Third, we
provide a full-scale synthetic evidence package with eight experiment families:
future-context coverage, estimated update-channel stress, nonlinear locality,
interface cost, stronger baselines, infeasibility/nullspace diagnostics,
manipulator-inspired synthetic tasks, and ablations. Fourth, we report honest
failure boundaries. UBC is exact only for the modeled local channel; noisy
Jacobian estimates, missing future contexts, wrong cost metrics, and nonlinear
curvature can all break the certificate.

\section{Related Work}

\paragraph{Learning from demonstration and correction.}
Learning from demonstration converts human traces into policies, trajectory
models, keyframes, or reward-like objectives
\citep{argall2009survey,akgun2012keyframe,chernova2014robot}. Physical
correction methods use human interaction as richer feedback about what the robot
should do next \citep{bajcsy2017learning,bajcsy2018learning,bobu2020feature}.
This paper does not claim novelty in using physical corrections as feedback. It
instead separates two questions that are often coupled: whether a correction
fixes the current behavior, and whether the robot's update rule can convert that
correction into future behavior change.

\paragraph{Preferences, coactive learning, and value alignment.}
Preference-based and coactive methods use rankings or improvements to infer
reward functions \citep{jain2015learning,sadigh2017active,wirth2017survey,
christiano2017deep}. Cooperative and pragmatic value-alignment approaches model
the interaction between human and robot as informative behavior
\citep{hadfieldmenell2016cirL,fisac2017pragmatic}. MEC is compatible with these
methods but lives at a lower interface layer. If a physical correction is mapped
into reward or policy parameters through some update channel, then MEC asks which
physical correction crosses a future boundary after that projection.

\paragraph{Shared autonomy and intervention learning.}
Shared autonomy blends human commands with robot inference
\citep{javdani2015shared,losey2018review}. Intervention-aided imitation learning
uses takeovers or corrections as data \citep{ross2011dagger,kelly2019hgdagger}.
These systems motivate the present diagnostic: an intervention can be helpful in
the moment while remaining weak or ambiguous as a future training signal. UBC is
not an autonomy arbitration policy; it is a certificate for the future effect of
a correction under a local update model.

\paragraph{Minimum intervention and task relevance.}
The minimum intervention principle in motor control emphasizes correcting only
task-relevant dimensions \citep{todorov2004optimality}. We borrow the spirit but
change the relevant space. Task relevance is not just a property of the current
state; it is filtered by the learner. A correction dimension is future-relevant
only if it survives the composed update channel into future margins.

\paragraph{Boundary to prior robot correction work.}
Prior work can be very close if it explicitly identifies the map from physical
correction to future behavior and optimizes correction effort through that map.
The novelty here is not the optimizer alone. The core novelty is the estimand:
minimum physical effort after projection through the update channel, with
infeasibility and nullspace treated as first-class diagnostic outputs.

\section{Problem Formulation}

Let $\theta\in\R^p$ denote the learner state: reward weights, policy features,
planner parameters, latent preferences, or another internal object. During an
interaction trajectory $\tau$, the human applies a physical correction
$c\in\R^d$. The robot update is
\begin{equation}
    \theta^+ = U(\theta,\tau,c).
\end{equation}
For a finite future context set $\mathcal{Z}=\{z_j\}_{j=1}^k$, define margins
$m_j(\theta^+)$ where $m_j(\theta^+)\ge \gamma$ means that the updated robot
passes the desired future predicate in context $z_j$. The future contexts can be
waypoints, obstacle cases, tactile thresholds, shared-autonomy states, or
representative scenes selected by a validation protocol.

\begin{definition}[Minimum Effective Correction]
Given a positive definite correction-cost matrix $W\succ0$, the Minimum
Effective Correction is
\begin{equation}
    c^\star =
    \arg\min_c \|c\|_W^2
    \quad \textrm{s.t.}\quad
    m_j(U(\theta,\tau,c))\ge \gamma,\quad \forall z_j\in\mathcal{Z}.
    \label{eq:mec}
\end{equation}
If no feasible $c$ exists inside the modeled physical correction channel, the
interface cannot certify the requested future behavior at this state.
\end{definition}

The definition is intentionally learner-relative. Infeasibility does not say the
task is impossible, the human is mistaken, or no teaching method can work. It
says the present interface and learner update cannot certify the requested
future behavior from the available correction coordinates. This is a useful
diagnostic: the right response may be a different teaching interface, richer
future contexts, better features, tactile sensing, or explicit preference
queries.

\paragraph{Local model.}
UBC uses the local affine approximation
\begin{equation}
    U(\theta,\tau,c) \approx \theta + Jc,
\end{equation}
where $J=\partial U/\partial c$ is the correction-to-update channel. Linearizing
future margins gives
\begin{equation}
    m_j(\theta+Jc) \approx a_j^\top \theta + a_j^\top Jc - b_j.
\end{equation}
Stacking rows yields
\begin{equation}
    Hc\ge h,\quad H=AJ,\quad h=b+\gamma\mathbf{1}-A\theta.
    \label{eq:channel}
\end{equation}
The matrix $H$ is the object that determines whether physical corrections can
change the chosen future behaviors.

\paragraph{Reachability.}
A future request is reachable if the halfspace system $Hc\ge h$ has a solution
within the physical correction cap. It is unreachable if no such correction
exists. The cap may represent force limits, human comfort, safety, or actuator
limits. In this paper, unreachable examples are not treated as failures of the
human. They are diagnostic outputs of the robot interface.

\section{Update-Boundary Correction}

UBC solves the local MEC problem
\begin{equation}
    c^\star = \arg\min_c c^\top W c
    \quad\textrm{s.t.}\quad Hc\ge h.
    \label{eq:weighted}
\end{equation}
The Euclidean case is $W=I$. The weighted case captures anisotropic interfaces:
some directions may be more uncomfortable, less safe, or harder to command.

\begin{proposition}[Local correction certificate]
For the affine update and linear margin model in Equation~\ref{eq:channel}, if
$W\succ0$ and the feasible set $C=\{c:Hc\ge h\}$ is nonempty and closed, then
Equation~\ref{eq:weighted} has a unique minimizer. This minimizer is the
minimum-cost physical correction for the modeled channel. If $C$ is empty, no
correction in the modeled channel can certify all specified future margins.
\end{proposition}

\paragraph{Algorithm.}
For the small correction dimensions used here, UBC is implemented by exact
active-set enumeration in low-dimensional cases and by a deterministic
constrained quadratic solve in higher-dimensional cases. The implementation is
not the contribution; the contribution is the diagnostic variable and the
interpretation of feasibility, nullspace, and cost. The same objective can be
solved by off-the-shelf QP solvers in a robot stack.

\paragraph{Nullspace warnings.}
If $v\in\mathrm{Null}(H)$, then $H(c+v)=Hc$ for every candidate correction $c$.
Thus $v$ can change the physical motion, contact force, or immediate compliance
without changing any certified future margin. Nullspace directions are where
current repair and future learning most visibly diverge.

\begin{proposition}[Learning-null effort]
Let $v\in\mathrm{Null}(H)$. For every feasible correction $c$, the correction
$c+v$ has identical modeled future margins and weakly larger cost when
$v^\top W c\ge 0$. In particular, if $c$ is the $W$-orthogonal projection onto
the feasible set, then adding any nonzero $W$-orthogonal nullspace component is
strictly wasteful for the certified future behaviors.
\end{proposition}

\paragraph{Estimated channels.}
In deployment, the robot may have only an estimate $\hat H$. If UBC solves
$\hat Hc\ge h$ but the true future margins use $H$, false certificates are
possible. A simple guard solves $\hat Hc\ge h+\rho\mathbf{1}$; this can reduce
false certificates, but it gives up exact minimum correction. The full-scale
experiments quantify this tradeoff.

\paragraph{Nonlinear recentering.}
When the local linear model is only approximate, a correction can satisfy
$Hc\ge h$ but fail the true nonlinear margins. We evaluate a trust-recentered
variant that uses a smaller local radius and a guard to mimic one-step online
validation. It is not claimed to solve nonlinear robot learning; it shows how
the certificate should degrade when the local approximation is stressed.

\section{Full-Scale Experimental Design}

The v3 suite is synthetic by design. It isolates the correction-channel
mechanism without confounding it with hardware sensing, user modeling, or
learned high-dimensional policies. Each trial samples a future-margin/update
matrix $H$, a required margin vector $h$, and sometimes an estimated channel
$\hat H$ or nonlinear true margin. Reachable trials are generated from a hidden
feasible correction and then solved without revealing that correction to the
method. Unreachable trials contain contradictory or rank-deficient future
constraints.

\paragraph{Families.}
Family A sweeps future-context coverage, correction dimension, nullspace
probability, and baselines. Family B stresses estimated update channels with
noise, scale bias, finite-sample quality, and guard margins. Family C evaluates
nonlinear locality and trust-recentered correction. Family D changes the
physical cost metric. Family E compares stronger correction-learning baselines.
Family F measures infeasibility and nullspace diagnostics. Family G adds
manipulator-inspired synthetic tasks. Family H performs ablations and negative
controls.

\paragraph{Metrics.}
For reachable requests, we report future success, current-margin success,
physical norm, false certificates, rank, and nullspace dimension. For
unreachable requests, we report detection. A false certificate means the method
returns a correction that appears valid to its internal model but fails the true
future margins. This metric is crucial for estimated-channel and nonlinear
settings.

\paragraph{Baselines.}
The suite includes current-only correction, one-axis current correction,
aggregate-gradient line search, random feasible search, least-squares repair,
soft margin-penalty repair, sequential greedy repair, reward-gradient correction,
coactive-style direction correction, robust guarded UBC, safe large guard, wrong
cost metrics, random future contexts, and oracle anchors. These baselines are
not intended to exhaust all preference-learning systems; they are stress
probes for the estimand.

\paragraph{RAM-light implementation.}
The runner writes summary rows and generated artifacts, not full dense matrices
for every trial. Families run sequentially, and progress is written to
\path{results/full_scale/progress.json}. The final package contains 2,426
seed-row summaries, eight figures, nine tables, metadata, and a report.

\subsection{Estimated Update Channels}

Table~\ref{tab:channel} attacks the strongest hidden assumption: the robot must
know the update channel. At $\sigma=0.10$ in the scale-matched,
32-sample-quality slice, unguarded estimated-channel UBC succeeds in only 0.344
of reachable cases and produces false certificates in 0.656. A guard of one
noise unit raises success to 0.906 and lowers false certificates to 0.094, but
the mean norm rises from 0.627 to 0.748. This is the expected tradeoff. Guarding
makes the correction more conservative and therefore no longer exactly minimum
for the estimated channel.

\section{Discussion}

\paragraph{What the positive result means.}
The positive result is not that a simple QP is a complete robot teaching system.
It is that the correction should be evaluated in the learner's future-behavior
coordinates. When $H$ is trusted, the projected halfspace problem tells the robot
which physical correction is minimally effective, which requests are unreachable
through the current interface, and which directions are learning-null.

\paragraph{What the negative results mean.}
The negative results are as important as the positive ones. Estimated channels
produce false certificates unless guarded. Nonlinear curvature breaks the raw
local certificate. Wrong cost metrics return corrections that are minimum in the
wrong physical units. Random context sets can certify the wrong future. These
are not implementation footnotes; they are the boundaries of the MEC claim.

\paragraph{How a robot should use the diagnostic.}
A deployed robot should not simply return $c^\star$ and trust it blindly. It
should also report the rank of the future-margin channel, the active future
contexts, the guard margin used for an estimated channel, the predicted margin
after correction, and whether the request is unreachable. If the correction is
near a nullspace direction, the robot should ask for a different teaching
modality or a more informative future-context set.

\section{Limitations}

The evidence is synthetic. There is no hardware robot, no real tactile data, no
learned high-dimensional policy, and no user study. The future context set is
designer-specified. The update channel is either trusted or synthetically
estimated. The nonlinear stress is a controlled model of curvature, not a real
policy-learning failure. The cost metrics are stylized rather than biomechanical.

These limitations are not incidental; they are the claim boundary. The paper is
ready as a mechanism/counterexample submission. It should not be presented as a
robotics systems result. A stronger systems paper would need learned update
Jacobians, real physical corrections, richer policy classes, and a context
selection protocol that is not hand-authored.

\section{Conclusion}

Physical correction papers often conflate immediate repair with future robot
learning. Minimum Effective Correction separates them. By projecting physical
effort through the robot's update channel, UBC identifies the smallest
correction that can change future behavior under a local model and exposes when
the interface cannot provide such a correction. The full-scale synthetic suite
supports the mechanism while also documenting the boundaries: estimated
channels, nonlinear locality, cost metrics, nullspaces, and future-context
coverage determine whether a correction is actually effective.

\section{Proof Details}
\label{app:proof}

\subsection{Projection Proof}

Let $C=\{c:Hc\ge h\}$. If $C$ is nonempty, it is an intersection of closed
halfspaces and is therefore closed and convex. Because $W\succ0$, the function
$f(c)=\frac12 c^\top Wc$ is strongly convex and coercive. A coercive continuous
function attains a minimum on a nonempty closed set, and strong convexity gives
uniqueness. Thus Equation~\ref{eq:weighted} returns the unique minimum-cost
correction in the local model.

If $C$ is empty, then no correction vector satisfies the modeled future-margin
constraints. This is a statement about the channel $H=AJ$ and context set
$\mathcal{Z}$, not about the task in general. A different learner, feature map,
future-context set, or teaching interface can change $H$ and therefore change
reachability.

\subsection{Weighted Transformation}

Let $W=L^\top L$ with $L$ invertible. Setting $y=Lc$ gives $c=L^{-1}y$ and
$c^\top Wc=\|y\|_2^2$. The constraint becomes $HL^{-1}y\ge h$. Thus the weighted
problem is a Euclidean projection in transformed coordinates. The experiments
use diagonal $W$, but the proof applies to any positive definite cost.

\subsection{Nullspace Result}

For any $v\in\mathrm{Null}(H)$, the modeled future margin vector after $c+v$ is
$H(c+v)-h=Hc-h$. Thus future certification cannot distinguish $c$ and $c+v$.
If $v$ is nonzero and $W$-orthogonal to the feasible projection direction, then
$\|c+v\|_W^2=\|c\|_W^2+\|v\|_W^2>\|c\|_W^2$. This proves that nullspace effort is
wasteful for the certified future margins even if it changes the current
physical trajectory.

\subsection{Channel-Error Bound}

Suppose UBC solves $\hat Hc\ge h+\rho\mathbf{1}$ but the true channel is
$H=\hat H+\Delta$. The true margin is
\begin{equation}
    Hc-h = \hat Hc-h+\Delta c.
\end{equation}
If $\|\Delta_i\|_2\le \epsilon$ for every row and $\|c\|_2\le r$, then each true
margin is at least $\rho-\epsilon r$. Therefore a sufficient guard is
$\rho\ge \epsilon r$. The condition is conservative because it uses a worst-case
row bound, but it explains the empirical tradeoff in Table~\ref{tab:channel}:
larger guards reduce false certificates by increasing the correction norm.

\subsection{Nonlinear Locality}

Let the true future margin be
\begin{equation}
    \tilde m_j(c)=H_jc-h_j+q_j(c),
\end{equation}
where $q_j(0)=0$ and $q_j$ has local curvature bounded by
$|q_j(c)|\le \kappa \|c\|_2^2$. A linear certificate with slack $s_j=H_jc-h_j$
is guaranteed only if $s_j\ge \kappa\|c\|_2^2$. Raw UBC often returns a point on
the boundary with small slack, so curvature can create false certificates.
Trust-region and recentering variants increase slack or reduce norm; they do
not remove the need for local validation.

\section{Algorithmic Details}

\subsection{UBC Solver}

For correction dimension at most three and modest future-context counts, the
runner enumerates active sets. At a Euclidean projection onto a polyhedron in
$\R^d$, the optimum has at most $d$ independent active halfspaces unless the
origin is feasible. Enumerating active sets is therefore exact up to numerical
tolerance in those cases. Higher-dimensional settings use a deterministic
constrained quadratic solve through \texttt{scipy.optimize} with linear
constraints. The paper does not depend on this solver choice.

\subsection{Baseline Details}

\paragraph{Current-only.}
This baseline projects onto the first future margin only. It models a correction
that repairs the present salient error but ignores the rest of the future
context set.

\paragraph{One-axis current.}
This baseline chooses the largest coordinate of the first margin and corrects
only that axis. It models a low-bandwidth interface or a teacher who can push
along one obvious direction.

\paragraph{Gradient line search.}
This method moves along the aggregate violated-margin gradient and searches over
step sizes up to the physical cap. It can work when all margins are aligned but
spends norm inefficiently when constraints point in different directions.

\paragraph{Random feasible search.}
Random search samples correction directions inside the norm cap and keeps the
smallest feasible candidate. It is a strong existence baseline but not an
efficient minimum-correction method.

\paragraph{Soft margin penalty.}
This baseline optimizes a differentiable penalty for violations plus a norm
penalty. It can return plausible directions while still failing exact future
constraints.

\paragraph{Sequential greedy repair.}
This baseline cycles through violated constraints and projects onto one margin
at a time. It is useful but order-dependent and not globally minimum.

\paragraph{Robust and guarded variants.}
Guarded UBC solves a stricter margin problem. It can improve true success under
estimated channels, but the resulting correction is no longer the exact minimum
for the unguarded local channel.

\section{Additional Interpretation}

\subsection{Why Current Repair Fails}

Current-only repair optimizes the first visible margin. That margin can be the
present obstacle, current waypoint, or most salient tactile threshold. Future
learning, however, is certified over a set of contexts. If the first row of $H$
is not representative of the rows that matter later, then the current correction
can be orthogonal to future learning. This is why current-only and no-future
context ablations collapse in Tables~\ref{tab:main} and~\ref{tab:ablation}.

\subsection{Why Random Search Is Not The Answer}

Random search often finds feasible corrections in low-dimensional reachable
sets, but it does not solve the minimum-correction problem. In the main setting,
random search has high future success but uses 2.259 times the UBC norm. In
robot teaching, that norm is not just a mathematical cost. It can mean extra
force, user discomfort, longer physical intervention, or an unnecessarily large
trajectory deviation.

\subsection{Why Infeasibility Matters}

Without an infeasibility output, a robot can keep asking for stronger physical
corrections even when the current interface cannot encode the future concept.
UBC changes the dialogue. It can say: this correction channel cannot move the
specified future margins; please provide a different modality, a new example, a
feature label, or a richer future-context set. This is especially important in
shared autonomy, where takeovers may solve the current state but fail to teach
future autonomy.

\subsection{Why The Cost Metric Matters}

The Euclidean metric is convenient for simulation, but physical correction has
anisotropic costs. A human may be comfortable pushing laterally but not upward;
a tactile correction may be cheap along a compliant axis and expensive along a
stiff axis; a shared-control joystick may have different gains by direction. MEC
therefore includes $W$ in its definition. Reporting only Euclidean norm hides
the interface assumptions.

\section{Worked Example}

This section gives a concrete small example that illustrates why the MEC object
differs from current repair. Consider a two-dimensional physical correction
$c=(c_1,c_2)$. The current visible error is represented by the first row
$H_1=(1,0)$ and required margin $h_1=0.2$. A current-only correction returns
$c=(0.2,0)$ with norm 0.2. Now suppose the future context set also includes
$H_2=(0,1)$ with $h_2=0.5$ and $H_3=(1,1)$ with $h_3=0.8$. The current-only
correction satisfies the present row, but its future margins are
$H_2c-h_2=-0.5$ and $H_3c-h_3=-0.6$. It is physically small because it optimizes
the wrong feasible set.

UBC instead solves the intersection of all three halfspaces. One feasible point
is $c=(0.3,0.5)$, and the projection of the origin onto the polyhedron is the
minimum correction under the chosen cost. The difference is not that UBC likes
larger corrections. It is that the minimum is computed after specifying what the
robot must learn for future contexts. The present correction is smaller only
because it ignores future rows.

Now add a learning-null direction. Let the physical correction be
$c=(c_1,c_2,c_3)$ but let every future row have zero third component. A person
can apply a large $c_3$ that changes the current contact force or trajectory
shape. The future margin vector is unchanged because $H(c+\alpha e_3)=Hc$. In a
real robot, this can correspond to pushing along a compliance direction that the
learner does not encode, moving a waypoint in a feature that is not part of the
reward, or applying tactile force that is filtered out by preprocessing. MEC
does not call that effort useless in every sense; it calls it learning-null for
the specified future predicates.

Finally, consider an unreachable request. If the future set contains
$H_1=(1,0)$ with $h_1=0.5$ and $H_2=(-1,0)$ with $h_2=0.5$, then no physical
correction can satisfy both. A correction system without infeasibility detection
may ask for a larger intervention. UBC instead reports that the current channel
cannot certify the request. The correct next action is to change the context
set, representation, teaching interface, or learner, not to demand more force.

\section{Future-Context Selection Protocol}

The guarantee in this paper is conditional on the finite context set
$\mathcal{Z}$. This is a limitation, but it is also a design handle. A robot
should not use a single current state as the only future context unless the
deployment problem truly has no future variation. A practical context protocol
should include at least five categories.

\paragraph{Task-critical contexts.}
These are states in which the corrected behavior is essential: obstacle
approaches, grasp-precontact states, narrow passages, human-proximity states,
or high-force tactile phases. If these contexts are absent, the correction can
repair an easy state while failing the one that motivated the intervention.

\paragraph{Near-boundary contexts.}
Future margins near zero are the contexts where a small update can flip behavior.
Sampling only easy positive or easy negative cases makes the halfspace problem
look stable while missing decision boundaries. UBC is most informative when
$\mathcal{Z}$ includes near-boundary examples.

\paragraph{Diverse nuisance contexts.}
The corrected behavior should be tested across irrelevant variations: object
pose, approach side, speed, load, contact friction, or user command. These
contexts prevent the correction from being effective only in the narrow current
state.

\paragraph{Counterfactual contexts.}
Some future contexts should ask whether the correction changes the wrong
behavior. For example, a correction meant to avoid a left obstacle should not
make the robot avoid every goal on the left. Counterfactual contexts can expose
overgeneralization.

\paragraph{Interface-diagnostic contexts.}
These contexts are chosen to reveal rank and nullspace problems in the physical
correction channel. If adding them increases the rank of $H$, the robot learns
that the original future set was underdiagnosed. If the rank remains low, the
current interface may be structurally unable to teach the requested concept.

Family A and Family H are synthetic versions of this protocol. Family A expands
the number of future contexts and nullspace probabilities. Family H removes the
future context set or replaces it with random contexts. The collapse of the
no-future and random-context ablations demonstrates why context selection is not
an afterthought. It is part of the correction certificate.

\section{Estimated Update-Channel Protocol}

A real robot rarely knows $J=\partial U/\partial c$ exactly. It may estimate the
channel from finite differences, replay buffers, demonstrations, tactile
responses, or learned local models. The deployment protocol should therefore
separate three objects: the estimated channel $\hat H$, the validation channel
used after a correction, and the guard policy used between them.

\paragraph{Finite-difference estimation.}
A simple procedure perturbs the correction interface along basis directions,
applies the learner update, and measures changes in future margins. This yields
a local estimate of each column of $H$. The procedure is expensive if physical
rollouts are required, but it directly estimates the object UBC needs. It also
reveals anisotropic uncertainty: some correction directions may be measured much
more reliably than others.

\paragraph{Model-based estimation.}
If the learner update is differentiable, the robot can compute $J$ analytically
or by automatic differentiation. The future margins still require a map $A$ from
updated parameters to future behavior predicates. A differentiable planner or
policy can provide this map locally, but discontinuous planners may require
finite differences or smoothing.

\paragraph{Replay estimation.}
Corrections stored in a replay buffer can estimate the channel statistically.
This is attractive because it avoids new perturbations, but it introduces
selection bias: the buffer contains corrections humans chose under earlier
policies, not necessarily directions needed now. Family B's scale and sample
stresses are simple proxies for this issue.

\paragraph{Guard selection.}
A guard margin should depend on uncertainty and norm. The sufficient condition
in the proof section suggests $\rho\ge \epsilon r$ under a rowwise error bound.
In practice, the robot can estimate $\epsilon$ from validation residuals and
choose $r$ from the proposed correction norm. Larger guards reduce false
certificates but can reject reachable requests or increase effort.

\paragraph{Online validation.}
After proposing a correction, the robot should validate predicted future margins
under a held-out context set or a recentered local model. If validation fails,
the system should either increase the guard, recenter the linearization, or
report uncertainty. The false-certificate metric in Families B and C is meant to
force this behavior into the evaluation.

\section{Nonlinear Validation Protocol}

The local affine model is useful only near the correction point. A real robot
should therefore treat UBC as a local proposal, not an unconditional global
certificate. A practical validation loop has four steps.

First, compute the minimum correction under the current linear model. Second,
predict the future margins and active constraints. Third, simulate, roll out, or
differentiate the updated policy at the proposed correction. Fourth, either
accept the correction, add a guard, or recenter the local model around a smaller
intermediate correction.

The key quantity is not only success but certificate mismatch. A mismatch occurs
when the linear model predicts all margins nonnegative but the true nonlinear
evaluation fails. In Family C, the raw linear certificate has mismatch rate
1.000 once curvature is nonzero in the reported slice. This intentionally harsh
stress prevents the paper from overclaiming local theory as global behavior.
The trust-recentered variant improves success by reducing the mismatch, but it
still leaves false certificates at high curvature. That is the correct outcome:
nonlinear validation helps, but does not remove the need for real model checks.

This protocol also clarifies when UBC should refuse to return a crisp answer.
If the local model changes rapidly across small recentering steps, the robot
should report that the update channel is unstable. If the active future contexts
change discontinuously, the context set may be near a planner mode switch. If
the correction norm required for feasibility exceeds the locality radius, the
robot should not claim a local certificate even if the QP is feasible.

\section{Baseline Fairness Audit}

The baselines are chosen to test different alternatives to the MEC estimand.
They are not all equally strong optimizers, and the paper should be explicit
about what each one can and cannot prove.

\paragraph{Current repair baselines.}
Current-only and one-axis current are intentionally weak for future behavior.
Their role is to show that immediate repair is not sufficient. They should not
be used as the only comparison in a final systems paper, but they are necessary
negative controls for this mechanism paper.

\paragraph{Search baselines.}
Random search is a strong feasibility baseline in low-dimensional settings. If
random search succeeds, then the feasible set exists and the question becomes
minimum effort. In the main setting random search nearly matches success but
uses more than twice the norm, supporting the minimum-correction claim.

\paragraph{Gradient and penalty baselines.}
Gradient line search and margin-penalty descent represent common relaxations:
move in a plausible improvement direction, or penalize violations softly. These
methods can produce useful corrections when margins are aligned, but they can
also produce false certificates because they do not enforce every future
boundary exactly.

\paragraph{Guarded baselines.}
Safe large guard and robust UBC are strong baselines because they solve stricter
problems. They can match success under trusted channels, but their norms are
larger. This is not a failure of those methods; it shows the tradeoff between
minimum correction and conservative robustness.

\paragraph{Oracle anchors.}
The oracle anchor is a reachability witness, not a minimum correction. It proves
that the trial was generated with a feasible hidden correction. UBC often finds
a smaller correction than the anchor, demonstrating that witness feasibility and
minimum effectiveness are different objects.

\section{Structured Task Details}

The manipulator-inspired family is deliberately synthetic, but each task encodes
a robotics pattern.

\paragraph{Planar pushing.}
The correction has two coordinates, and future margins represent pushing
directions that keep an object away from obstacle boundaries. This task has low
rank problems only rarely, so UBC and weighted UBC tend to agree.

\paragraph{Shared waypoint correction.}
The correction has three coordinates, and margins represent future trajectory
features after a shared-autonomy update. Some physical directions are
overrepresented because joystick or waypoint interfaces often have asymmetric
gains.

\paragraph{Force/tactile threshold correction.}
The correction has four coordinates. Later coordinates have lower update
sensitivity and higher cost, representing tactile channels that are physically
available but weakly encoded by the learner. This task stresses the difference
between Euclidean and weighted costs.

\paragraph{Peg-compliance correction.}
The correction has five coordinates with deliberately low-rank components.
Some force directions change compliance in the current insertion but do not
alter future margins. This task is the structured analogue of the nullspace
diagnostic.

None of these tasks is hardware. Their purpose is to make the abstract channel
model less sterile while preserving control over ground-truth reachability and
false certificates.

\section{Failure Taxonomy}

The suite exposes recurring failure modes that a real system should report.

\paragraph{False current sufficiency.}
The current margin is fixed, but future margins remain negative. This is the
dominant failure for current-only correction and no-future-context ablations.

\paragraph{False channel certificate.}
The estimated channel says the correction is feasible, but the true channel
does not. This is the main failure in Family B. Guarding reduces the rate by
paying norm.

\paragraph{False local certificate.}
The linear model says the correction is feasible, but nonlinear validation
fails. This is the main failure in Family C. Trust recentering helps but does
not eliminate it.

\paragraph{Wrong metric minimum.}
The correction is minimum under the wrong effort model. This failure does not
necessarily reduce success, but it changes human cost and therefore changes the
meaning of "minimum."

\paragraph{Nullspace waste.}
The correction has components that do not affect future margins. This is
especially likely in low-rank tactile or compliance interfaces.

\paragraph{Context misspecification.}
The correction certifies the wrong future set. Random contexts or missing
counterfactual contexts can make an update appear effective while deployment
contexts remain unsafe.

\section{Reporting Checklist}

A paper or robot system using MEC-style correction should report the following:

\begin{itemize}
\item the physical correction coordinates and cost matrix;
\item the learner update channel or estimation method;
\item the future context set and how it was selected;
\item reachable future success and unreachable detection separately;
\item false certificate rate under estimated or nonlinear channels;
\item correction norm under the actual interface cost;
\item rank and nullspace statistics of the composed channel;
\item guard margins and validation protocol;
\item ablations without future contexts and without update-channel projection;
\item limitations on hardware, user intent, nonlinear global behavior, and
context coverage.
\end{itemize}

The v3 artifact reports these quantities in synthetic form. A systems follow-up
would need to report the same checklist on real correction data.

\section{Reviewer Self-Attacks}

\paragraph{This is just a QP.}
The optimization is intentionally simple. The paper's claim is not that a small
QP is algorithmically novel. The claim is that physical corrections should be
evaluated through the future update channel, and that feasibility/nullspace
diagnostics should be part of the robot's response.

\paragraph{Synthetic evidence is not enough for robotics.}
Correct for a systems claim. The paper is scoped as a mechanism and diagnostic
paper. Hardware validation remains future work.

\paragraph{Future contexts are hand selected.}
Correct. The guarantee is conditional on $\mathcal{Z}$. A deployment system
needs a context selection protocol. The paper makes this explicit and tests
negative controls where random or incomplete contexts fail.

\paragraph{The update channel may be unknown.}
Correct. Family B is devoted to this failure. The result is not ``UBC is robust
for free''; the result is that guards and validation are required when $H$ is
estimated.

\paragraph{Human intent is not inferred.}
Correct. MEC assumes future predicates are specified. It is complementary to
intent inference and preference learning, not a replacement for them.

\section{Deployment Roadmap}

A real robot implementation would need four additional pieces. First, it would
need a learned or estimated update channel from physical correction to learner
state. Second, it would need a future-context sampler tied to deployment risk.
Third, it would need a cost model calibrated to the correction interface and
human comfort. Fourth, it would need online validation after applying a
correction. UBC can then serve as the local diagnostic inside a larger teaching
system: propose the minimum effective correction when reachable, reject
unreachable requests, and identify nullspace directions that cannot teach the
future behavior.

\section{Acceptance Criteria For A Real System}

This mechanism paper should not be treated as a systems result until a real
robot satisfies stronger acceptance criteria. The criteria below are written as
a checklist because they are easy to blur in narrative form.

\paragraph{Criterion 1: correction-channel calibration.}
The robot must estimate the composed channel from physical correction to future
margin. Reporting only a policy update loss is insufficient. The system should
measure whether predicted margin changes match held-out future contexts after
the physical correction is applied. A calibration curve should plot predicted
margin improvement against realized margin improvement, with separate curves for
small and large correction norms. If calibration error grows with norm, the
robot should reduce the locality radius or recenter before claiming a
certificate.

\paragraph{Criterion 2: reachable and unreachable labels.}
A benchmark should contain both reachable and unreachable correction requests.
If every request is reachable, a method can avoid the diagnostic problem by
always returning some correction. If no requests are unreachable, infeasibility
detection cannot be evaluated. Unreachable labels do not require knowing that a
task is impossible in the world; they require knowing that the chosen interface
and learner cannot certify the requested future margins within the physical cap.

\paragraph{Criterion 3: future-context holdout.}
The contexts used to solve UBC should be separate from contexts used for final
evaluation. Otherwise, a method can overfit the finite context set. The holdout
set should include near-boundary cases, nuisance variations, and
counterfactuals. The central metric should be future-context success on the
holdout set, not current-state repair.

\paragraph{Criterion 4: physical effort measured in the real interface.}
The effort metric must match the interface. For kinesthetic teaching this may
include force, torque, duration, and comfort. For shared autonomy it may include
joystick displacement or takeover time. For tactile correction it may include
contact pressure and direction. A method that is minimum in Euclidean feature
space but uncomfortable in the physical interface has not solved MEC for the
robot.

\paragraph{Criterion 5: false certificate accounting.}
Every estimated-channel or nonlinear experiment should report false
certificates. A correction that is returned as feasible but fails true future
margins is more dangerous than an explicit rejection, because it gives the user
confidence that learning occurred. Family B and Family C include false
certificate rates for exactly this reason.

\paragraph{Criterion 6: interface redesign trigger.}
If UBC repeatedly reports infeasibility or high nullspace dimension, the robot
should change the teaching interface instead of repeatedly asking for larger
corrections. This is a design criterion, not just a metric. The diagnostic is
valuable only if the system acts on it.

\section{Real-Robot Transition Design}

The most direct hardware follow-up is not to deploy UBC on a high-dimensional
policy immediately. A more controlled transition would use a manipulator with a
small correction interface and a known update rule, then gradually remove
assumptions.

\paragraph{Stage 1: controlled waypoint learner.}
The robot executes trajectories parameterized by a small waypoint or feature
vector. A human physically corrects one segment. The learner updates waypoint
features through a known or estimated linear map. Future contexts are held-out
start-goal-obstacle configurations. This stage tests the core distinction
between current repair and future policy margins while keeping the update
channel interpretable.

\paragraph{Stage 2: tactile threshold learner.}
The robot performs contact-rich tasks with tactile thresholds, such as wiping,
insertion, or sliding along a surface. Corrections are forces or impedance
changes. The update channel maps force corrections into future threshold or
impedance parameters. This stage tests anisotropic costs and nullspace effort,
because some force directions are physically salient but weakly encoded by the
learner.

\paragraph{Stage 3: shared-autonomy intervention learner.}
The user takes over a shared-control interface. The learner updates policy
features from intervention segments. Future contexts are held-out autonomy
states in which the learned policy should avoid similar failures. This stage
tests whether UBC can explain when a takeover corrected the moment but failed to
teach future autonomy.

\paragraph{Stage 4: learned channel with online validation.}
Only after the previous stages should the robot learn $H$ from data. The system
should maintain uncertainty estimates, choose guard margins, and validate
predicted future margins after correction. A meaningful comparison would report
unguarded UBC, guarded UBC, robust UBC, preference-learning baselines, and
current-only intervention learning on the same held-out future contexts.

The stages are deliberately modest. They respect the current paper's evidence
boundary while giving a path from mechanism to system. The first real-robot
success criterion is not outperforming every correction-learning method. It is
showing that the projected future-boundary diagnostic predicts when physical
corrections actually change future autonomous behavior.

\section{What Would Falsify The Mechanism}

A useful mechanism paper should state what evidence would weaken it. Several
outcomes would challenge the practical value of MEC.

\paragraph{Current repair always predicts future success.}
If, in a realistic correction benchmark, current-only repair were perfectly
correlated with future-context success across diverse contexts, then the
projection through $H$ would add little value. This would not disprove the math,
but it would make the diagnostic unnecessary for that benchmark.

\paragraph{The update channel is too unstable to estimate locally.}
If small physical corrections produce discontinuous or high-variance future
updates, then local UBC may be unusable without a different learner. The correct
conclusion would be that the robot's correction interface is poorly conditioned,
not that the user should push harder.

\paragraph{Human cost is dominated by factors outside the model.}
If comfort, fatigue, attention, or safety costs cannot be approximated by a
useful local metric $W$, then minimum-correction claims should be weakened. The
robot may still use UBC as a feasibility diagnostic, but not as a human-effort
optimizer.

\paragraph{Future contexts cannot be specified.}
If the system cannot identify meaningful future predicates, then MEC lacks a
target. In that case, intent inference, preference elicitation, or task
specification must come first. UBC assumes future margins; it does not invent
them.

\paragraph{Alternative interfaces dominate.}
If language feedback, demonstrations, or preference queries consistently provide
lower-cost and more reliable future updates than physical corrections, then the
robot should use those interfaces. MEC is learner-relative and interface-relative;
it can diagnose that physical correction is the wrong teaching channel.

\section{Submission Boundary And Reviewer Guidance}

The v3 paper should be reviewed as a mechanism paper. Its main contribution is
the distinction between physical correction size and future-effective correction
size after the learner's update projection. It should not be reviewed as a
hardware robot paper, a tactile sensing paper, or a new preference-learning
architecture.

The strongest positive evidence is the combination of exact local theory and
wide synthetic stress. Family A verifies the main effect over future-context
coverage. Family B shows estimated-channel fragility and guard tradeoffs. Family
C prevents overclaiming local linear theory. Family D makes the cost metric
explicit. Family E compares against stronger correction-learning probes. Family
F tests infeasibility and nullspace diagnostics. Family G adds embodied
structure without pretending to be hardware. Family H shows that removing future
contexts or update-channel projection breaks the mechanism.

The strongest remaining weakness is the absence of real robot validation. The
paper should therefore avoid phrases such as ``solves physical correction'' or
``teaches future behavior'' without the qualifier ``under the modeled local
channel.'' The submission-safe version is: UBC gives a local certificate for
whether a physical correction can change specified future margins through the
robot's update channel, and it exposes when current repair is not future
learning.

\section{Artifact-To-Claim Audit}

The repository artifacts are organized so that each claim in the paper can be
checked against a generated file rather than only against prose. The main
coverage claim is supported by
\path{results/full_scale/family_a_context_coverage_seed.csv} and
\path{results/full_scale/table_main_context_coverage.tex}. Those files support
the statement that UBC solves reachable local future-context requests in the
main setting and that current-only correction fails the future set. They do not
support a claim about hardware, human comfort, or global nonlinear policies.

The estimated-channel claim is supported by
\path{results/full_scale/family_b_channel_seed.csv} and
\path{results/full_scale/table_channel_stress.tex}. These artifacts support the
statement that solving on $\hat H$ can produce false certificates and that guard
margins trade norm for improved true-margin success. They do not prove that any
particular real Jacobian estimator is accurate, nor do they identify the best
guard policy for a robot.

The nonlinear-locality claim is supported by
\path{results/full_scale/family_c_nonlinear_seed.csv}. It supports the statement
that a boundary-tight linear correction can fail under curved true margins and
that recentering-style conservatism can recover part of the success rate. It
does not support global nonlinear optimality.

The cost-metric claim is supported by
\path{results/full_scale/family_d_interface_cost_seed.csv}. It supports the
statement that the word ``minimum'' is metric-relative. It does not provide a
validated human biomechanics model. A real system must still calibrate $W$.

The stronger-baseline claim is supported by
\path{results/full_scale/family_e_strong_baselines_seed.csv}. It supports the
statement that several plausible correction directions do not recover the full
future-boundary certificate. It does not exhaust every preference-learning,
inverse-reinforcement-learning, or shared-autonomy baseline.

The infeasibility/nullspace diagnostic claim is supported by
\path{results/full_scale/family_f_infeasibility_nullspace_seed.csv}. It supports
the statement that contradictory future requests should be rejected and that
low-rank channels can waste physical effort in learning-null directions. It does
not claim that a rejected request is impossible for all teaching modalities.

The embodied-structure claim is supported by
\path{results/full_scale/family_g_embodied_seed.csv}. It supports the statement
that the mechanism can be instantiated in manipulator-inspired synthetic
settings. It does not claim real manipulation success.

The ablation claim is supported by
\path{results/full_scale/family_h_ablation_seed.csv}. It supports the statement
that future contexts and update-channel projection are necessary ingredients in
the synthetic suite. It does not prove that the exact same ablations would have
the same magnitudes on hardware.

This audit is intentionally conservative. The paper's evidence is strongest
where the artifacts are directly generated from the local channel model. The
paper's evidence is weakest where deployment would require perception,
user-intent inference, learned update Jacobians, human effort models, and
hardware contact dynamics.

\section{Final Audit Paragraph}

The full-scale runner completed with stage \texttt{complete}, 2,426 seed-row
summaries, and zero plot failures. The manuscript imports generated v3 figures
and tables from \path{results/full_scale/}. The final claim is local and
learner-relative: UBC identifies minimum effective corrections for the modeled
future-margin/update channel. The paper does not claim real-robot validation,
global nonlinear optimality, solved human intent inference, or a learned
high-dimensional tactile policy.
